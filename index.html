About

As an aspiring clinical neuroscientist and speech-language pathologist, my research focuses on advancing individualized neuromodulation and neuroimaging approaches for post-stroke aphasia and other neurogenic communication disorders. My work is driven by a central goal: to overcome key clinical barriers in rehabilitation by developing precision-based, neuroscience-informed interventions that can improve communication outcomes for people affected by neurological injury.

Drawing on years of clinical experience in adult medical settings, I have worked closely with individuals with aphasia, neurogenic communication disorders, and dysphagia. These frontline experiences continue to shape my research vision and reinforce my commitment to bridging cutting-edge neuroscience with real-world clinical practice, so that innovative discoveries in brain stimulation and neuroimaging can translate into meaningful benefits for patients and families.

Research

Brain Stimulation

Developing individualized connectomic tACS to target dysfunctional oscillatory networks in post-stroke aphasia, guided by patient-specific EEG dysfunctome mapping.

Oscillatory Connectomics

Investigating EEG oscillatory networks underlying language processing in aphasia using graph theory, network-based statistics, and cross-frequency coupling analysis.

Aphasiology

Advancing assessment and intervention for aphasia through discourse norms research, including establishing Cantonese norms of connected speech measures for clinical use.

Precision Neurorehabilitation

Pioneering individualized neuromodulation systems that combine EEG-guided brain mapping with patient-specific stimulation protocols for precision aphasia treatment.
